Unprecedented 'Crimson Glow' Algal Bloom Poses Health Risk Along Mumbai Coast

Authorities Issue Urgent Warning for Residents and Fishermen; Beach Access Restricted from Colaba to Juhu

Published: September 20, 2025 | By: Mumbai Coastal Times Staff

MUMBAI â€“ Coastal authorities have issued a high-alert warning today following the rapid expansion of a rare and potentially toxic Harmful Algal Bloom (HAB) along a significant stretch of Mumbai's coastline. The phenomenon, nicknamed the 'Crimson Glow' by locals, presents a dual nature: a stunning, electric-blue bioluminescent display at night and a murky, reddish-brown tide during the day.

The bloom, identified by scientists at the Maharashtra Institute of Oceanography (MIO) as a species of dinoflagellate known as Pyrodinium bahamense, was first spotted as small patches near the shores of Versova three days ago. However, a combination of unusually high sea surface temperatures ($29.5^\circ C$) and nutrient runoff from recent monsoon spells has caused an explosive growth, now stretching over 40 kilometers from the shores of Colaba to Juhu Beach.

"This is not the harmless 'neeli machli' or bioluminescence we occasionally see," warned Dr. Ananya Sharma, a leading marine biologist at MIO. "While the nighttime glow is captivating, the daytime reality is a dense concentration of algae that can produce potent neurotoxins. These toxins can become aerosolized in the sea spray, leading to respiratory irritation, coughing, and sneezing for people on the shore. Direct contact with the water can cause severe skin rashes."

The Brihanmumbai Municipal Corporation (BMC) has acted swiftly, closing public access to all major beaches including Girgaon Chowpatty, Dadar, Juhu, and Versova. Red flags and warning signs have been erected, and police patrols are advising morning walkers and tourists to stay clear of the shoreline.

Impact on Marine Life and Fishing Community

The most immediate and visible impact has been on local marine life. Reports of dead fish washing ashore have increased significantly in the last 24 hours. The dense bloom depletes oxygen levels in the water, creating hypoxic "dead zones" where fish cannot survive. Furthermore, the toxins accumulate in shellfish like clams and oysters, making them extremely dangerous for human consumption.

The Fisheries Department has issued a temporary ban on all inshore fishing and shellfish harvesting until the bloom subsides and the water is declared safe. "Our livelihoods are gone overnight," said Rajesh Patil, a fisherman from the Versova Koliwada. "The water stinks, and the fish we do find are dead. We have never seen a 'lal paani' (red water) this bad in our lifetimes."

Official Public Safety Advisory

Citizens are strongly advised to adhere to the following safety precautions:

  • DO NOT enter the ocean water for swimming, wading, or any recreational activities.
  • AVOID the immediate shoreline, especially during high tide or windy conditions which can increase sea spray.
  • Coastal residents, particularly those with respiratory conditions like asthma, should keep windows and doors closed.
  • DO NOT consume any local shellfish or fish caught in the affected area until the ban is lifted.
  • Keep pets away from the beach and prevent them from drinking or playing in the sea water.
  • If you experience respiratory irritation, eye irritation, or skin rashes after being near the coast, move inland and consult a doctor if symptoms persist. For emergencies, dial 112.

Scientists at MIO are continuously monitoring the bloom's density and trajectory using satellite imagery and water sample analysis. The current forecast suggests the bloom may persist for another five to seven days, depending on changes in wind patterns and ocean currents. Officials are hoping for a shift in offshore winds to help disperse the dense algal mass away from the coast.
